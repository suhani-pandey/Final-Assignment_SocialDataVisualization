Pattern 1

Geography — Where Do
Billionaires Live?

Does the country you're born in decide your chances of becoming a billionaire? The short answer is yes — more than almost any other factor. But the full picture is more interesting than just counting who has the most.

Why does one country have far more billionaires than another?

The simplest answer: bigger economy, more billionaires. The US has 724 billionaires — 27% of the entire world — because it has the biggest economy on the planet, the deepest stock markets, and the most mature tech industry. When a country generates more money overall, more of it ends up in fewer hands at the top.

But it's not just about being rich as a country. Look at India and France — they have roughly similar-sized economies, yet India produces 7 times more billionaires. Why? Because India has extreme inequality: a small group can capture enormous wealth while most people remain poor. France, with its stronger social safety net and wealth taxes, distributes money more evenly.

Small countries can punch above their weight too. Singapore has only 6 million people but 26 billionaires, because its low 17% tax rate and status as a global financial hub make it a magnet for the wealthy. Switzerland works the same way — it's not just a rich country, it's a country designed to attract and keep wealth.

The real lesson: three things together — a large economy, fair legal systems, and low corruption — create the conditions where billionaires can appear. Take away any one of them, and the count drops sharply.[7]

Pattern 1 — The Bottom Line
Where you live is one of the strongest predictors of whether you can become a billionaire.

Geography is not destiny, but it's close. The US, China, and a handful of small financial hubs produce the overwhelming majority of the world's billionaires — not because people there are smarter or work harder, but because those places have the economic infrastructure that turns hard work into extreme wealth. For most of the world, that infrastructure simply doesn't exist yet.

Pattern 2

Become Billionaires?

We often imagine billionaires as young geniuses who struck gold overnight. The data tells a very different story. Most billionaires are old — and they got rich slowly.

Why are most billionaires old? And why are young billionaires so rare?

Think of wealth like a snowball rolling down a hill. The longer it rolls, the bigger it gets. Most billionaires didn't wake up rich one day — they spent 20 to 30 years slowly building a business, reinvesting profits, watching their company grow, and eventually reaching a point where the numbers added up to a billion. That process simply takes time. The typical billionaire is 65 years old for exactly this reason.

Young billionaires like Mark Zuckerberg or Kylie Jenner make headlines precisely because they're exceptions, not the rule. They had a rare combination of the right idea, the right timing, and often significant existing connections or resources. Of the 2,640 billionaires in 2023, fewer than 60 are under 40.

Here's the surprising twist though: being older doesn't make you richer. We might expect the oldest billionaires to be the wealthiest — more time to compound — but the data shows wealth is spread fairly evenly across age groups. A 45-year-old and an 80-year-old billionaire have roughly similar net worths on average. What age really shows is how long you've been in the game, not how big the game got.[4]

Pattern 2 — The Bottom Line
Getting rich takes time. There is no shortcut to a billion dollars.

The average billionaire spent decades building their wealth before making the Forbes list. Young tech billionaires get all the media attention, but they represent less than 2% of the total. If there is one pattern that holds across countries, industries, and backgrounds, it's this: extreme wealth is almost always the result of patience and compounding over a very long time.

Pattern 3

Life Expectancy — Does Living in
a Healthier Country Help?

Here's something unexpected: countries where people live longer tend to produce more billionaires per person. But it's not about health — it's about what living longer signals about a country's stability and fairness.

Why do countries where people live longer tend to have more billionaires?

At first glance this sounds strange — what does how long people live have to do with billionaires? But life expectancy is really a shortcut measure for how well a country functions. Countries where people live long lives tend to also have stable governments, fair legal systems, low corruption, and reliable infrastructure. Those same things — not the long lives themselves — are what allow wealth to be built and kept.

Think about it this way: if you build a successful business in a country with weak rule of law, the government could seize it, a rival could steal it, or the economy could collapse. Your wealth is always at risk. In a country with strong institutions, your wealth is protected and can compound safely over decades.

But life expectancy alone is not the full answer. Japan is the perfect example of why. Japan has the highest life expectancy in the world — people regularly live into their 90s — yet it has very few billionaires per million people. Japan also has high taxes, a culture that discourages extreme visible wealth, and a business environment that favours large corporations over individual founders. So the conditions for long life are there, but the conditions for billionaires are not.

The outliers at the top — Singapore and Hong Kong — combine long lives with something extra: extremely low taxes and a deliberate policy of attracting global wealth. That combination puts them far above the trend line.[7]

Pattern 3 — The Bottom Line
A country's life expectancy is really a measure of how safe and stable it is — and that stability is what billionaires need.

Countries where people live long, healthy lives also tend to be the places where wealth can be safely built and kept over generations. Life expectancy doesn't create billionaires directly — but it signals the kind of environment where extreme wealth becomes possible. The one clear exception is Japan, which proves that long lives alone aren't enough: you also need a system that rewards and protects individual wealth creation.

Why do Finance and Tech produce the richest people? And why do more billionaires in a sector actually mean less wealth per person?

Finance produces the richest billionaires on average because of how the money flows. A fund manager who looks after $50 billion in assets charges roughly 2% per year as a fee — that's $1 billion per year just for managing money, before any returns. The scale of capital in finance is simply unlike any other industry. You don't need to make a product, hire a factory, or ship anything. You just need to be trusted with other people's money.

Technology is close behind, and growing fast, because of one special property: it costs almost nothing to serve one more customer. A software product built once can be sold to ten million people with almost no extra cost. That's why a company like Facebook or Google can become worth hundreds of billions — their product scales infinitely. No factory, shop, or restaurant can do that. This is why companies built after 1970 (when computers and the internet began) produce the wealthiest founders — they had access to a kind of scaling that simply didn't exist before.[4]

Here's the surprising flip side: the more billionaires an industry has, the lower the average wealth per person. Finance and Tech have many billionaires, but also many "merely" wealthy ones. Smaller industries with fewer total billionaires — like Metals & Mining — can still produce very rich individuals because the wealth isn't spread as thin. More people sharing a pie means smaller slices, even if the pie itself is large.

Finally: building your own company beats buying or inheriting one. When you buy an existing business, you pay today's full price. When you build one from scratch, you capture all the growth from day one — which over 20–30 years becomes the difference between millions and billions.[3]

Pattern 4 — The Bottom Line
If you want to become very rich, your choice of industry matters enormously — but so does timing.

Finance and Technology are the two clearest paths to extreme wealth because they both allow one person to control or capture value at a massive scale. But the data also shows that companies founded after 1970 produce far wealthier billionaires on average — the internet changed the rules. A brilliant business founded in 1950 simply couldn't grow the way a tech platform built in 1995 could. The lesson: the right industry at the right moment in history is one of the most powerful advantages a founder can have.

Why do most billionaires build their own wealth? And why are there so few women?

Most billionaires — 70% — built their own wealth from scratch. This might feel inspiring, and in some ways it is. But it also reflects a simple economic reality: starting a company early gives you the longest possible runway for your wealth to grow. A founder who starts with nothing and builds a business over 30 years captures every dollar of growth. Someone who buys an existing business pays the full price upfront and captures only future growth. The math strongly favours building over buying.

Here's the twist though: inherited billionaires are individually richer, on average. The typical person who inherited their wealth has $2.1 billion, compared to $1.5 billion for the typical self-made billionaire. Why? Because inherited wealth starts compounding from a very high base — often from a previous generation's already-enormous fortune. It's hard to outrun a billion-dollar head start, no matter how hard you work.[4]

Now for the gender gap — and it's a big one. Only 11.6% of billionaires are women, and just 30% of female billionaires built their wealth themselves, compared to 75% of men. This is not because women are less capable or less ambitious. The data points to several practical barriers: women receive far less startup funding from investors (who are mostly men, investing in networks they know); the ages when people typically start companies — 25 to 45 — overlap heavily with the years when women disproportionately carry caregiving responsibilities at home; and most female billionaires entered the list through inheritance from a husband or father, not through independent wealth creation.[6]

The good news is that the gap is closing, slowly. In Technology and Media, women's self-made rates are much closer to men's — suggesting these barriers are not permanent or universal, but something that can be changed with the right conditions.

Pattern 5 — The Bottom Line
Building your own wealth works — but the game is not equally open to everyone.

The rise of self-made billionaires is a real trend and reflects genuine entrepreneurial energy across the world. But the gender data is a clear reminder that access to that path is not equal. Women face real, practical barriers — in funding, in timing, in the networks that capital flows through. The gap between 75% male self-made and 30% female self-made is not about ambition. It's about access.

Why has the number of billionaires grown so fast — and why does it keep going up?

In 1996, there were 423 billionaires in the world. By 2023, there were 2,640 — more than six times as many in less than 30 years. So what happened? Three things, happening at the same time.

First, the stock market made existing rich people much richer. When stock prices and property values rise year after year — as they mostly have since the 1990s — anyone who already owns a lot of shares or real estate sees their wealth grow automatically, without doing anything new. Many people crossed the billion-dollar mark simply because the assets they already owned went up in value.[4]

Second, the internet created a completely new kind of business — one that could reach billions of customers worldwide with almost no extra cost per user. One great app, built once, can run on a billion phones. This kind of scaling was simply impossible before computers. It's why Technology went from barely existing on the billionaire list in 1996 to being the single largest sector by 2023.

Third, the world got bigger — at least economically. In 1996, the billionaire list was mostly American and European. By 2023, China had 539 billionaires and India had 169. As those economies grew rapidly, they created enormous amounts of new wealth — and some of it concentrated, as it always does, at the very top.[3]

Trend Analysis — The Bottom Line
The billionaire class didn't just grow — it was transformed by technology and globalisation.

The world of billionaires in 2023 looks almost nothing like 1996. It's larger, more global, more tech-driven, and slightly more self-made. The forces behind this growth — rising asset prices, internet-scale businesses, and emerging market economies — show no sign of slowing down. If anything, they are accelerating. The question isn't whether there will be more billionaires by 2030, but how many — and from where.

Surprising Findings

Three Things We Didn't Expect

The most interesting part of any analysis is when the data tells you something you didn't see coming. Here are three patterns that genuinely surprised us — and what they say about how extreme wealth really works.

Surprise #1
More education doesn't automatically mean more billionaires

We assumed that countries with the most university graduates would produce the most billionaires. The data says: not quite.

Fig. 12 · Interactive Scatter Plot — Hover for country details
University Enrollment Rate vs Billionaires per Million People

Fig. 12 What this shows: Each circle is one country. X-axis = share of young people enrolled in university (%); Y-axis = billionaires per million residents; circle size = total billionaire count. How to read it: A country in the top-right has both high education and many billionaires per person. Hover to see the country name. Key finding: There is a loose connection (r ≈ 0.4) but many highly-educated countries — Finland, South Korea, Sweden — have very few billionaires per capita. Education alone is not enough.

So what's actually going on?

Education helps — but it's not the deciding factor. Finland sends over 90% of its young people to university, yet has only a handful of billionaires. Meanwhile, the US, which has similar university rates, has 724. The difference isn't education — it's the size of the market, the depth of the financial system, and the culture around starting companies and taking risks.

Think of education like the soil in a garden. Good soil helps plants grow, but you also need seeds, water, and sunlight. A highly educated population gives a country the potential for billionaires — but without large markets, easy access to startup capital, and investor networks, that potential never fully develops.[7]

And what about the famous dropouts — Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, Mark Zuckerberg? They are the exception, not the rule. What's easy to miss is that they dropped out of Harvard and Stanford — the most elite educational environments in the world. They were surrounded by brilliant, ambitious people and had access to world-class resources. Dropping out of a top university is very different from never going at all. Their success came despite dropping out, not because of it.[5]

Surprise #2
Even among billionaires, a tiny few own almost everything

We knew billionaires were richer than most people. We didn't expect them to be so unequal among themselves.

Fig. 13 · Lorenz Curve

Fig. 13 What this shows: The blue curve shows how wealth is distributed among billionaires, ranked from poorest to richest. So what's actually going on?

When we say "billionaire," we picture someone with unimaginable wealth. And that's true. But what the Lorenz curve shows is that even inside this already-extreme group, the gap between the "poorest" billionaire and the richest is staggering.

The typical (median) billionaire is worth about $1.6 billion. That sounds enormous — and it is. But compare it to Elon Musk, Jeff Bezos, or Bernard Arnault, who are worth hundreds of billions. The difference is so large it almost doesn't fit in human intuition. The "average billionaire" is a statistical fiction — no one is really average. Most are clustered near the bottom of the scale, while a tiny handful sit so far above everyone else that they pull the average up dramatically.[4]

Why does this happen? Because the very top billionaires own companies that don't just compete in their market — they dominate it globally. Amazon, Tesla, LVMH, and Meta are each worth hundreds of billions of dollars. When you own a large share of something that size, ordinary billionaire wealth can't keep up. The gap between a $1.6B fortune and a $200B fortune is not a matter of working harder. It's a matter of owning the right company at the right scale at the right time.[2]

Surprise #3
The month you were born in has a tiny but measurable effect

This one sounds almost too strange to be true. But the numbers don't lie — and the explanation is surprisingly human.

Fig. 14 · Bar Chart
How Many Billionaires Were Born in Each Month?

Fig. 14 What this shows: Each bar = the number of billionaires born in that month. The dashed line = the number you'd expect if birth months had no effect at all (total ÷ 12 = roughly 220 per month). How to read it: Bars above the dashed line mean that month produced more billionaires than chance would predict. Key finding: January and October stand out above the baseline. A statistical test confirms this pattern is not random (p < 0.05).

So what's actually going on?

This is called the relative age effect, and it has been found in many other places — elite sports, academic success, even CEO appointments. Here's the simple version: in most countries, children start school based on a cutoff date, often January 1st. A child born in January is almost a full year older than a child born in December in the same school year. At age 6 or 7, that age gap is significant — the older child seems smarter, more confident, and more capable.

Teachers notice. Coaches notice. Those children get more encouragement, more practice, more opportunities. Small advantages stack up over years. By the time those children become adults, the original age gap has long disappeared — but the confidence, the habits, and the head start remain.[2]

Author Malcolm Gladwell wrote about exactly this pattern in his book Outliers, focusing on elite hockey players. The billionaire data suggests the same effect reaches all the way to the top of the wealth ladder. Being born in January doesn't guarantee anything — but it gives you a slightly better chance of being spotted, encouraged, and set on a path that compounds over decades into something extraordinary.

One important nuance: being born in January doesn't make you richer once you become a billionaire — the sizes of January fortunes and December fortunes are similar. The effect is on the probability of getting there, not the destination.

Surprising Findings — The Bottom Line
Becoming a billionaire is shaped by forces most people never think about.

Education, individual effort, and good ideas all matter — but so do the size of your country's financial markets, the luck of being born into the right era, and even something as random as your birth month. The data shows that extreme wealth is not purely a reward for individual talent. It is also a product of systems, timing, and compounding advantages that start very early in life and build invisibly over decades.

What do these predictions actually mean — and how much should we trust them?

These forecasts are based on the trends we've seen from 1996 to 2023. We used a mathematical method (polynomial regression) to extend those trends forward to 2030, and we calculated a confidence band — the shaded area on each chart — to show the range of plausible outcomes. Think of the dashed line as the most likely path, and the shaded zone as the "probably somewhere in here" zone.

The count forecast (~3,400 by 2030) will likely be roughly right if the economy keeps growing, stock markets stay healthy, and no major global crisis interrupts things. A big recession, a major war, or sweeping new wealth taxes could push the real number lower. A new wave of AI-driven businesses could push it higher.

The self-made rate (~72%) is the most stable forecast because it's been rising slowly and steadily for decades. It's unlikely to jump dramatically in either direction over just seven years.

The female share (~13.7%) is the most troubling forecast. Even if everything goes well, women will still represent only about 1 in 7 billionaires by 2030. That's 19 years of very slow progress — from 5.2% in 1996 to a projected 13.7% in 2030. The barriers are real and they don't fix themselves quickly.

One more finding worth noting: we used a machine learning model to predict whether any given billionaire would be self-made or inherited, based on their industry, age, country, and gender. The model was right 68% of the time, and the single most important predictor was industry — not gender, not country, not age. What field you work in shapes your path to wealth more than almost anything else.[2]

Future Predictions — The Bottom Line
The billionaire class will keep growing — but for women and most of the world, the pace of change remains frustratingly slow.

By 2030, there will likely be around 3,400 billionaires. More of them will have built their own wealth. More will come from China, India, and emerging markets. But women will still only be about 1 in 7, and the vast majority of countries will still produce no billionaires at all. Growth at the top is real — but it is not evenly shared.

What We Learned

So — What Does It Actually Take
to Become a Billionaire?

After studying 2,640 billionaires across 76 countries and nearly 30 years of data, five clear patterns emerged. None of them are what most people expect.

Where you're born matters more than almost anything else.

The single biggest predictor of whether someone becomes a billionaire is their country. Not their intelligence, not their work ethic — their geography. Countries with large economies, stable laws, and deep financial markets produce nearly all the world's billionaires. Most of the planet simply doesn't have the infrastructure for extreme wealth creation yet.

It takes decades — and patience is not optional.

The typical billionaire is 65 years old. They didn't get rich quickly. They spent 20 to 30 years building something, reinvesting, compounding, and waiting. The "overnight success" stories we see in the news are genuine outliers — rare exceptions that prove the rule rather than breaking it.

The industry you choose is your single biggest career decision.

Finance and Technology produce the richest billionaires because they both allow one person to control enormous value at scale. Software built once can serve a billion users. A fund managing $50 billion earns fees no factory could match. Choosing to work in one of these fields — especially before they became dominant — is one of the most powerful advantages a founder can have.

Building your own company works — but access to that path is unequal.

70% of today's billionaires built their own wealth. That's a genuine shift over the past 30 years. But the gender data is a clear warning: only 30% of female billionaires are self-made, compared to 75% of men. The opportunity to build wealth is real — but it is not equally available to everyone, and the gap won't close on its own.

Extreme wealth compounds — and inequality compounds with it.

Even within the billionaire class, a tiny few own almost everything. The top 10% of billionaires hold 50% of all billionaire wealth. Once you're at the very top, wealth grows by itself — stocks rise, assets appreciate, companies dominate global markets. The gap between a "regular" billionaire and the richest people alive is almost as hard to imagine as the gap between billionaires and the rest of us.

The forces creating billionaires are accelerating, not slowing down.

In 1996, there were 423 billionaires. By 2023, there were 2,640. By 2030, there will likely be around 3,400. Rising asset prices, internet-scale businesses, and rapid growth in China and India are all still running full speed. The world will have more billionaires in the future — the question is whether the benefits of that wealth creation will reach further down, or concentrate even further at the top.

Becoming a billionaire is not simply a matter of working hard or having a great idea. It is the result of geography, timing, industry, patience, and a series of compounding advantages — many of which begin long before anyone starts a company. Understanding those patterns doesn't make extreme wealth inevitable or fair. But it makes it legible. And that, ultimately, is what data is for.
